# PE 606 - Findings

## Correct result
- `S(10^36)` last nine digits: `158452775`.

## Problem structure
- Let `G(n)` be the number of gozinta chains for `n`.
- A gozinta chain is equivalent to an ordered factorization of `n` into factors `> 1`.
- For `n = \prod p_i^{e_i}`, `G(n)` depends only on the exponent multiset `(e_i)`.
- Solving `G(n)=252` over exponent patterns gives exactly one pattern: `(3,3)`.
- Therefore all valid numbers are exactly `n = p^3 q^3` with distinct primes `p < q`.

## Sum reduction
- For general `N`:
  - `S(N) = \sum_{p<q,\,(pq)^3 \le N} (pq)^3`.
- Let `X = \lfloor N^{1/3} \rfloor`. Then:
  - `S(N) = \sum_{p<q,\,pq\le X} p^3 q^3`
  - `= \sum_{p\le\sqrt{X}} p^3\,(P_3(X/p)-P_3(p))`, where `P_3(t)=\sum_{r\le t,\,r\text{ prime}} r^3`.
- For `N = 10^36`, `X = 10^12`.
- This reduction also reproduces the checks:
  - `S(10^6)=8462952`
  - `S(10^12)=623291998881978`.

## Efficient computation
- Bottleneck: many `P_3(t)` queries with `t` up to `10^12`.
- Use a Min_25/Lehmer-style prime summatory method for `P_3`.
- Evaluate the reduced pair-sum formula and take modulo `10^9`.

## Wrong approaches and why they fail
- Treating `G(n)` as multiplicative or combining prime-power counts independently: gives wrong `G(n)=252` classification.
- Stopping at an empirical `p^3 q^3` pattern without excluding other exponent patterns: incomplete and unreliable.
- Naively enumerating semiprimes/primes up to `10^12`: infeasible runtime.
- Guessing a residue (for example `0`) without completing the prime-cube summation: invalid.
